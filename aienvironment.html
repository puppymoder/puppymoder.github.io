AI and The Environment, the Pros and Cons
AI usage has exploded in the past few years, with it, the computational power needed to train and run these models. AI data centres are popping up across the globe, requiring significant amounts of water and electricity to keep them running.
Having studied Environmental Science prior to Cyber Security, environmental issues are something I am passionate about, so I wanted to know, do the benefits of artificial intelligence in facing environmental challenges outweigh the costs?
Part 1 – The Cons
A Neural Network is a machine learning model, commonly used alongside Natural Language Processing to give us chatbots like ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude. It is estimated that training models such as these emits ~300,000kg of CO₂, to put this into context, the average car will emit 57,000 kg in its lifetime, five times less than training a single AI model [1]. This number will vary from model to model, however the impact is significant nevertheless.
In 2023 data centres consumed 4.4% of the total US electricity, this is expected to rise to between 6.7% and 12% by 2028 [2]. Between September 2023 and August 2024, US data centres produced over 105 million tons of CO₂e (carbon dioxide equivalent) with a carbon intensity 48% higher than the national average [3]. So alongside the massive cost of training an AI, the data centres used to keep them running emit huge amounts of CO₂, this is not helped by their inefficiency, with an emission intensity higher than the US average.
Water consumption is another hot topic regarding AI, whilst it may not reach the levels of industries such as agriculture and energy, the rapid expansion of AI and subsequent water usage is a major cause for concern. A 20-50 question ‘conversation’ with an AI may use up to 500ml of water, factoring in the millions of users daily, this adds up rapidly, further burdening an already strained resource [4]. AI’s projected water usage could reach 6.6 billion m³ by 2027 [5] highlighting the need for drastic improvements in cooling system efficiency. Whilst models for this do exist, it is ultimately up to organisations to implement these new systems.
Part 2 – The Pros
Despite the costs, AI is proving pivotal in addressing environmental challenges, being applied in a variety of sectors including natural resource conservation, waste management, clean energy, ecosystem monitoring, and many more [6].
AI’s ability to analyse and visualise complex datasets is already being utilised by world leading environmental agencies. UNEP launched the World Environment Situation Room (WESR) in 2022, leveraging AI to “curate, aggregate, and visualise the best available earth observation and sensor data to inform near real-time analysis and future predictions”, informing decisions and driving actions on global environmental policy [7]. The applications of this system are vast, land-use changes and deforestation can be analysed as they occur, pollution levels, including greenhouse gas emissions, can be easily evaluated to drive change in key areas, even natural disasters can be more reliably predicted to allow for timely action.
Additional uses of AI in managing environmental risks include identifying changes in icebergs, helping calculate the amount of meltwater released into the ocean due to climate change. The use of AI has increased the speed of this process by 10,000x the previous rate. In a similar vein to this, the Dutch environmental organisation ‘The Ocean Cleanup’ is using AI to identify ocean litter in remote locations, aiding in the collection and removal of ocean waste [8].
Conclusion
Artificial Intelligence is evolving rapidly, despite it’s widespread use we are still in the early stages of this technology. Despite this, it has proven hugely successful in the management of the environmental challenges we face, being successfully used in numerous sectors to combat these issues. Whilst it does create new challenges in terms of carbon emissions and water consumption, I believe that as our understanding of the technology and its requirements improve, these problems can be mitigated, and AI can continue to play a vital role in combating the environmental challenges we face.
